INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

This protocol can be used to titer lentivirus preparations that confer antibiotic
resistance. Determining the titer of your lentiviral preparation allows you to
control the multiplicity of infection (MOI) in downstream studies. This protocol
was developed for A549 cells but can be adapted to a variety of target cell lines
and selection markers. Note this assay requires staining resistant colonies with
crystal violet solution and, therefore, the cells cannot be used for later
experiments.

CONSIDERATIONS BEFORE YOU START
================================================================================

â€¢ The health of the target cell line is critical for obtaining accurate titers.

â€¢ Check the cells for mycoplasma regularly.

â€¢ Do not over or under-grow your cells.

â€¢ Thaw a new vial of cells after 20â€“30 passages.

â€¢ Do not add penicillin/streptomycin to the media, as it can affect viability and
  transduction efficiency.

â€¢ Titer will vary between cell lines.

â€¢ It is not recommended that lentiviral supernatants be subjected to multiple
  freeze-thaw cycles.

â€¢ This protocol outlines the seeding of the cells at the same time as the
  virus-mediated transduction. For some cell lines, transduction is optimized if
  the cells are seeded the day before transduction. Note that in that case,
  polybrene should only be added at the time of viral transduction, and not during
  the cell seeding step.


PROCEDURE
================================================================================

1. Before beginning a colony formation assay, the dose of antibiotic required to
   kill your target cell line needs to be empirically determined. Treat the
   target cells with a range of doses of antibiotic. Determine the minimum
   concentration required to kill all of the cells. Use this dose for the colony
   formation assay.

2. Prepare a batch of DMEM complete containing 10 Î¼g/mL polybrene by diluting 20
   Î¼L of 10 mg/mL polybrene into 20 mL of media.

3. If using freshly collected virus, filter through a 0.45 Î¼m polyethersulfone
   filter to remove cells and debris.

4. Lentiviral titer can decrease during cycles of freeze-thaw. If you are
   freezing and aliquoting virus, it is recommended that you titer from the
   frozen stock to account for any loss in titer associated with freeze-thaw.

5. If using frozen virus, thaw the lentiviral aliquot on ice prior to use.

6. Prepare 10-fold serial dilutions of the lentivirus into DMEM complete
   containing 10 Î¼g/mL polybrene:

   Dilution      | Volume of Virus/Previous | Volume of DMEM Complete | Volume of Virus | Volume of Cells | Final Viral
                 | Dilution (Î¼L)            | + Polybrene (Î¼L)       | Added (Î¼L)     | Added (Î¼L)     | Dilution
   --------------|--------------------------|-------------------------|-----------------|----------------|------------
   1:10          | 100 of Stock Virus       | 900                     | 150             | 1,350          | 1:100
   1:100         | 100 of 1:10              | 900                     | 150             | 1,350          | 1:1,000
   1:1,000       | 100 of 1:100             | 900                     | 150             | 1,350          | 1:10,000
   1:10,000      | 100 of 1:1,000          | 900                     | 150             | 1,350          | 1:100,000
   1:100,000     | 100 of 1:10,000         | 900                     | 150             | 1,350          | 1:1,000,000

   Mix the dilutions well.

   Note: the 1:10 dilution can usually be omitted because this dilution typically
   produces a lawn of cells, which cannot be accurately quantified.

7. Add 150 Î¼L of a viral dilution to each well (each well gets one dilution).

8. Also include an untransduced well by using 150 Î¼L of media in place of virus.

9. Seed 1,000 cells into each well of a 6-well dish.

   Prepare a batch of cells as follows: Dilute 7,000 cells into 9.45 mL of DMEM
   complete containing 10 Î¼g/mL polybrene.

10. Mix well by pipetting or inverting.

11. Aliquot 1.35 mL of the cell suspension into each well of the 6-well dish
    directly on top of the virus. Swirl gently to mix and place the dish in the
    incubator.

12. Incubate for 48â€“72 h.

    ðŸ’¡ PRO TIP: Different cells may be more sensitive to lentivirus or to
    polybrene, in which case this incubation step can be reduced to 8 h and the
    media can be replaced with DMEM complete. However, antibiotic-containing media
    should not be added until 48â€“72 h after transduction.

13. Gently aspirate the media from the cells.

14. Add 1.5 mL of DMEM complete containing the appropriate antibiotic.

15. Incubate the cells for ~2 weeks. All of the cells in the untransduced
    (negative) control well should be killed and no colonies should be visible.

16. Every 3â€“4 days, gently aspirate the media and replace it with fresh DMEM
    complete containing the appropriate antibiotic.

17. When the untransduced (negative control) cells have died and colonies are
    visible in the transduced wells, aspirate the media from the wells.

18. Gently wash the cells with 1 mL of PBS and aspirate wash.

19. Filter 0.1% crystal violet solution through a 0.22 Î¼m filter to remove any
    precipitates.

20. Stain each well with 1 mL of 0.1% crystal violet for 10 min at RT.

21. Gently remove the stain.

22. Wash cells 3x with 1 mL of PBS, being careful not to disturb the colonies.

23. Count the colonies for at least 2 of the dilutions.

    ðŸ’¡ PRO TIP: The higher dilution wells tend to provide more accurate titers as
    the likelihood of multiple integration events decreases as the dilution
    increases.

24. Calculate the transduction units per mL (TU/mL) as follows:

    TU/mL = number of colonies / total volume in the well (mL) Ã— dilution factor

    e.g., If the 1:100,000 well has 75 colonies, then there are 75 colonies /
    1.5 mL Ã— 1,000,000 or 50,000,000 TU/mL

    For a more accurate titer, take the average of multiple dilutions.
