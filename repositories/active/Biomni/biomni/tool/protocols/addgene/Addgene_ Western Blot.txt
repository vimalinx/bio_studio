INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Western blot is a technique used to separate proteins by size followed by
detection using antibodies specific to the protein of interest. This protocol
describes the basic steps for lysing cells, determining total protein
concentration in the lysate, running a precast SDS-PAGE gel, and immunoblotting.

General Guidelines

‚Ä¢ Acrylamide percentage varies in SDS-PAGE gels. Use lower percentage acrylamide
  when immunoblotting high molecular weight proteins and higher percentage
  acrylamide when immunoblotting low molecular weight proteins.

‚Ä¢ This protocol uses a dry transfer device but can be adapted for wet and
  semi-dry transfer approaches.

PROCEDURE
================================================================================

Section 1: Lyse cells

1. Centrifuge 5 x 10‚Å∂ cells for 5 min at 100 x g.

2. Gently remove supernatant.

3. Resuspend cell pellet in 1 mL of 1X PBS and transfer to a microcentrifuge
   tube.

4. Centrifuge for 5 min at 100 x g.

5. Carefully remove supernatant.

6. Resuspend cell pellet in appropriate volume of cold lysis buffer.

   üí° PRO TIP: The volume of lysis buffer will vary depending on the size of your
   cell pellet but will generally be between 250‚Äì1000 ¬µL.

   üí° PRO TIP: The ideal lysis buffer will vary depending on the cellular location
   of the protein of interest. RIPA buffer is suitable for most proteins but more
   stringent buffers and a sonication step may be required for hard to extract
   proteins such as those in the nucleus.

7. Incubate on ice for 30 min.

8. Centrifuge lysate for 15 min at 14,000 x g at 4 ¬∞C.

9. Transfer supernatant to a clean microcentrifuge tube.

10. Use the lysate immediately or store at -80 ¬∞C until ready to use.

Section 2: Determine the total protein concentration and prepare the lysate for
SDS-PAGE

1. Determine the protein concentration using a Pierce BCA Assay Kit or other
   preferred method for protein determination.

2. Prepare a 50:1 Reagent A to Reagent B dilution of the BCA assay.

3. Prepare serial dilutions of the BSA standard that range from 0‚Äì2000 ¬µg/mL.

4. In duplicate, dilute 10 ¬µL of standard, blank, and lysate samples into 200 ¬µL
   of BCA reagent in a 96-well microtiter plate.

5. Incubate for 30 min at 37 ¬∞C.

6. Determine the absorbance at 590 nm.

7. Calculate the average absorbance of the duplicate samples on the plate.

8. Subtract the average absorbance of the blank from all of the samples.

9. Plot a standard curve of BSA standard concentration versus absorbance.

10. Extrapolate the total protein concentration of the sample from the standard
    curve.

11. Determine the volume of sample required to load an equivalent amount of total
    protein for each sample.

    üí° PRO TIP: The ideal total protein loaded will vary between samples and
    target proteins but is typically between 10‚Äì50 ¬µg. If the protein is in low
    abundance in the sample you will need to load a greater amount of total
    protein.

12. Prepare sample for loading as follows:
    ‚Ä¢ Add 10% v/v Œ≤-mercaptoethanol to the 4X protein loading buffer.
    ‚Ä¢ Dilute 4X protein loading buffer in the sample to 1X.
    ‚Ä¢ Boil the samples for 10 min at 100 ¬∞C.

Section 3: SDS-PAGE

1. Prepare the precast gel as follows:
   ‚Ä¢ Remove the gel from the plastic packaging.
   ‚Ä¢ Remove tape and plastic comb.
   ‚Ä¢ Rinse the wells with deionized water 3x.
   ‚Ä¢ Shake the gel gently between washes to remove residual water.

2. Load the gel into the chamber of the SDS-PAGE gel tank.

   üí° PRO TIP: Different SDS-PAGE gel tanks will have different gel orientations.
   Refer to the instructions for your specific tank.

3. Close the clamp.

4. Prepare 1X running buffer as follows:
   ‚Ä¢ Dilute 25 mL of 20X running buffer to 500 mL with deionized water. Mix well.

5. Fill the chamber of the SDS-PAGE gel tank with the 1X running buffer.

6. Load the samples into the gel.

7. Load 5‚Äì10 ¬µL of the prestained protein ladder.

8. Place the lid on the tank and plug the electrode cords into the power supply.

9. Run the gel at 100 V for 10‚Äì15 min or until the samples have moved out of the
   wells and into the gel.

10. Increase the voltage to 150 V and continue running the gel until you have
    obtained the desired separation.

11. Gently open the gel with the spatula.

Section 4: Dry transfer

1. Soak the gel for 15 min in 20% ethanol in deionized water.

   To prepare 20% ethanol, dilute 2 mL of ethanol into 8 mL of deionized water
   and mix well.

2. Assemble the transfer sandwich as follows:
   ‚Ä¢ Unseal the iBlot 2 PVDF Mini transfer stack.
   ‚Ä¢ Set the Top Stack to one side and discard the white separator.
   ‚Ä¢ Keep the Bottom Stack in the plastic tray.
   ‚Ä¢ Place the Bottom Stack on the blotting surface.
   ‚Ä¢ Align the electrical contacts on the blotting surface of the iBlot 2 Gel
     Transfer Device.
   ‚Ä¢ Wet the pre-run gel in deionized water and place it on the transfer membrane
     of the bottom stack.
   ‚Ä¢ Soak a piece of iBlot Filter Paper in deionized water.
   ‚Ä¢ Place the pre-soaked iBlot Filter Paper on the gel and remove the air bubbles
     with the roller.
   ‚Ä¢ Place the Top Stack over the pre-soaked filter paper.
   ‚Ä¢ Remove air bubbles using the roller.
   ‚Ä¢ Place the Absorbent Pad on top of the Top Stack such that the electrical
     contacts are aligned with the corresponding electrical contacts on the
     blotting surface of the iBlot 2 Gel Transfer Device.

3. Close the lid of the device.

4. Select the desired method and make sure the parameters are correct.

5. Transfer for 5‚Äì6 min for proteins <30 kDa.

6. Transfer for 8‚Äì10 min for proteins >150 kDa.

7. Select Start Run.

8. When the run is complete, select Done.

Section 5: Blocking

1. Prepare 1X TBST as follows:
   ‚Ä¢ 25 mL of 20X TBS
   ‚Ä¢ 2.5 mL of Tween-20
   ‚Ä¢ 472.5 mL of deionized water
   ‚Ä¢ Mix well

2. Prepare blocking buffer as follows:
   ‚Ä¢ Dilute 5% w/v non-fat milk into 1X TBST
   ‚Ä¢ Example: 5 g of non-fat milk into 100 mL of 1X TBST.

   üí° PRO TIP: The ideal blocking buffer will vary between antibodies. Refer to
   the manufacturer's information before running your experiment.

3. Remove the membrane from the transfer device and place it in a container with
   1X TBST, protein side up.

4. Block the membrane in blocking buffer for 1 h at RT on a shaking platform.

5. Wash the membrane 3x for 5 min in 1X TBST at RT on a shaking platform.

Section 6: Antibody incubation

1. Dilute the primary antibody to the desired concentration in blocking buffer.

   üí° PRO TIP: The optimal concentration will vary between antibodies but is
   typically between 1‚Äì10 Œºg/mL.

2. Incubate the membrane overnight in primary antibody at 4 ¬∞C on a rocking
   platform.

   üí° PRO TIP: Primary antibody incubation can be reduced to 2 h at RT but may
   result in more non-specific binding.

3. Wash the membrane 3x for 5 min in 1X TBST at RT on a shaking platform.

4. Dilute the horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody to the desired
   concentration in blocking buffer.

   üí° PRO TIP: The optimal concentration will vary between antibodies but is
   typically between 1‚Äì10 Œºg/mL.

5. Incubate the membrane with secondary antibody for 60 min at RT on a shaking
   platform.

6. Wash the membrane 3x for 5 min in 1X TBST at RT on a shaking platform.

7. Prepare the chemiluminescence substrate by mixing 1:1 reagent A to reagent B.

8. Gently incubate the membrane in the chemiluminescence reagent for 5 min at RT.

9. Cover the membrane with clear plastic and use a gel imager with
   chemiluminescence detection or a dark room to detect the bands.


TIPS AND TROUBLESHOOTING
================================================================================

‚Ä¢ The optimal lysis buffer will vary depending on the sample type and cellular
  location of the protein of interest. You may need to try a variety of lysis
  buffers to find the best for your target.

‚Ä¢ The optimal concentration and blocking buffer will vary between antibodies.
  Review the instructions before starting your experiment and consider titrating
  your antibody to determine the optimal dose.

‚Ä¢ To ensure that your antibody is both functioning as expected and is specific,
  include a positive control sample that you know expresses the protein, such as
  cells transfected with the protein of interest, and a negative control sample
  that does not express the protein of interest.
